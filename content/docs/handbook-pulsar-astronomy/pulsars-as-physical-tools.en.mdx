One of the best things about this book is that it does not stop at "what a pulsar is". Chapter 2 asks a more ambitious question: what can pulsars be used for?

The answer is still impressive.

## The chapter's main claim

Pulsars are not only neutron stars. They are also measurement devices.

With precise timing and propagation measurements, they can act as probes of:

- time standards and rotational stability
- binary dynamics and celestial mechanics
- strong-field gravity
- planetary systems
- Galactic structure and magnetic fields
- neutron-star interiors
- plasma physics under extreme conditions

## Why timing sits at the center

Most of these applications begin with stable pulse arrival times. Once a source can be timed well, its spin, motion, orbit, and propagation path all become measurable in one framework.

For a timing-friendly pulsar, the same basic observables keep appearing:

$$
\tau_c = \frac{P}{2\dot P},
\qquad
\dot E \propto \frac{\dot P}{P^3}
$$

These are not perfect physical truths. They are compact, useful summaries derived from measured spin parameters, and the handbook is good at showing how much mileage astronomers get from them.

## What needs a historical reading

The chapter's examples remain instructive, but some of the "future" framing is now clearly historical. Pulsar timing arrays, double neutron star tests, and large survey forecasts have all moved on since 2005. The value of the chapter today is not in its exact predictions, but in how clearly it lays out the logic of why pulsars became tools for other branches of physics.

## Why this page belongs in the docs

If you only learn pulsar processing as a chain of dedispersion, folding, calibration, and TOA extraction, it is easy to miss the larger reason those steps matter. This chapter restores that reason.

## The applications are not all of the same kind

One useful way to read this chapter is to separate its applications into three layers. The first is clock-like use: stable pulse arrival times let pulsars compete with the best natural standards we know. The second is dynamical use: binary pulsars, astrometry, and Solar-System corrections turn timing residuals into measurements of motion, mass, and geometry. The third is environmental use: dispersion, rotation measure, and scintillation let pulsars probe the Galaxy and the interstellar medium rather than just the neutron star itself.

That three-layer view helps explain why the same object can appear in so many different literatures. A millisecond pulsar may be discussed in one paper as a timing standard, in another as part of a gravity test, and in a third as a line-of-sight probe through ionised Galactic plasma. The common ingredient is not that the underlying physics is simple. It is that the observables are unusually precise and unusually reusable.

## Where the historical framing matters most

The chapter's strongest historical value is in the way it anticipates later fields. Pulsar timing arrays, double neutron star tests, and neutron-star interior constraints were already visible as coherent research programs, even if the data landscape of 2005 was much thinner than today's. That is why the chapter still repays careful reading. It does not just list examples. It teaches the habit of asking what a pulsar measurement is actually constraining: a clock, an orbit, a field, a column density, a compact-star equation of state, or a plasma process.

For a documentation site, that matters because it restores motivation. GUI steps produce useful numbers, but this chapter reminds you why those numbers became important enough to build an entire observing discipline around them.
